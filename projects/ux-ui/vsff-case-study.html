Introduction

Since 2005, Vancouver Short Film Festival has been celebrating and promoting short films, music videos and animation videos with an aim to connect the emerging artists to the wider audience and established filmmakers.

As a student of UI/UX Strategy, I picked VSFF because I enjoy watching short films. As someone working in the creative field, I could relate to the process of filmmaking. When you are done editing a film or any other form of art, you have watched your film/art hundreds or even thousands of times. Artists, especially short filmmakers, start having second thoughts about their art. The level of confidence is usually low at this time.

Watching cinema is all about the experience. A film has to make you feel something. So should everything that is connected to films. Attending a film festival is a great experience but the website should also give a glimpse of that experience.

Beyond the experience, it should also be functional. The website should have an easy to understand the schedule of films being screened. It should have a clear and easy process for submitting the film for filmmakers. The website has to be mobile responsive as the audience or filmmakers won't always be on their computers while visiting the website.

This case study tries to analyze the current website of VSFF to identify any problems and inconsistencies that come in the way of users trying to accomplish their tasks.

The Challenge

The whole identity of the festival on the website feels very vague and lacks confidence. When looking at the websites of VIFF and tiff, it's very clear what those festivals stand for.

The logo, typography, layout and overall design of VSFF give the impression that the festival screens very amateurish films. However, the films that are screened at the festival are of very high quality. They are just as good as the films that are screened at tiff and VIFF. The only difference is VSFF screens short films.

Most people think of short films as something less than feature films which are not true. The website's unprofessional design and the lack of some basic features combined with most people's perception will make it difficult for the festival to reach its potential audience.

Here are some inconsistencies that I encountered and wanted to fix:

- The process of submitting the film takes the filmmaker to another website that only allows the uploading of the film.
- There is no easy to understand and navigate the schedule calendar that the audience can look at to know about the films being screened.
- There is a big banner on the landing page that doesn't look bad but also isn't exciting. The height of the banner makes it go beyond the whole screen of most computer screens. This, for a while, made me think that there isn't any more content after the banner.
- The website is not mobile responsive which makes the festival look less relevant in today's mobile-first digital age.

The Process

Pain Points

After collecting all the data, I identified some pain points. These were the things that really made the user think if he wants to continue using the website or not.

1. The user wants to submit the film just by a link. He doesn't want to re-upload the whole film every time he submits his film to a festival.

2. There is no easy to understand schedule calendar for the films being screened.

3. The website is not responsive at all. This ruins the audience's experience, especially when they are attending the festival and have to check the schedule on their phones.

4. The schedule is a long list of film titles with descriptions. There's too much scrolling.

Journey Map

REVISION: After a peer review session, I found that the journey map is less holistic. I didn't take into consideration the user's state of mind before he got to the website and after he had finished his goal on the website. Also, I didn't touch on the user's pain points.

From the feedback that I got from other students and the instructor, I expanded the journey map a little more to understand the user's state of mind before and after using the website.

Paper Prototyping

After collecting data from the website and understanding the user's behaviour, I picked up a pen and a paper to sketch a rough idea of how the website will behave and look.
So, I made a sketch of three of the website's pages i.e. home page, user dashboard page and the schedule calendar page. All these three pages were related to what the user wanted to do on the website.

Wireframes

To refine the paper sketches, I made the wireframes of the three pages. The idea in my head was starting to make more sense with this. There were things that made sense on the paper but weren't working on the wireframes. So, I made some adjustments.
